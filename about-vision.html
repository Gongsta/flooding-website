Our Vision

Floods are the most common and devastating type of natural disasters in the world. A recent report showed that in the last decade, over 2.3 billion people have affected by flooding events (Wallamacq et al. 2015). Floods’ occurrences represent 43% of all natural disasters (UNISDR 2015). Hence, it is paramount that humans develop innovative technologies to mitigate the effects of this devastating natural disaster. Early warnings can greatly prevent fatalities and economic damages as anticipated floods allow for precautions to be taken. As part of this process, accurate models need to be created in order to properly forecast and warn individuals of the risk in their area.

The traditional flood forecasting techniques can be broken down into two parts: measuring or computing the discharge of the river, and modelling the water’s hydraulic behavior in order to generate an inundation map. In the first part, a hydrograph can be built from the obtained discharge data which allow scientists to visualize how an area (ex: basin, river, etc.) reacts to events such as a period of rainfall (see Figure 1). To measure these discharge values, stream gauges are used. However, in many parts of the world, these instruments are either inexistent or out of use, forcing scientists to use other methods to compute the volume of water flowing at various points of a river. Currently, scientists mainly solve this problem by building a hydrological model.

Hydrological modelling allows us to build a complex representation of the physical processes in the environment in order to give us a detailed overview of how factors such as precipitation and soil moisture affect the flow of a given river. However, the problem lies in the fact that detailed hydrological models currently used are basin-specific, require enormous amounts of measurements which are often unavailable in remote parts of the world, need to be tuned manually, and are built using specialized software. All these factors mean that these models are not made to be scaled globally. The idea in this project to implement deep learning for flood forecasting is thus explored because of their ability to incorporate external variables for forecasting without the costly manual tuning for conventional hydrological models that is necessary to simulate accurate river discharge data, in the hopes of ultimately building a scalable global deep learning model that reduces the need of human intervention. This paper will demonstrate the implementation of a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model architecture which has been proven to work well on forecasting time-series data (Ogunmulu et al. 2016), and compared to the results of current hydrological model forecasts such as the Global Flood Awareness System (GloFAS) 30-day forecasts (Hirpa et al. 2018).

The second part of flood forecasting is generating a flood map which delineates the areas which will be affected. Traditionally, satellite imagery has been used in order to generate Digital Elevation Maps (DEMs), which provide an overview of the topography of the Earth. A hydraulic model can then be created by combining DEM data with river discharge data computed by the hydrological model to model the way water navigates the ground and map out which areas are at risk of flooding. However, traditional DEMs are known to be outdated and low in resolution, since they require specialized missions which means a great cost and cannot be funded to be updated every year. This project shows a simple yet novel methodology which removes the need to build a hydraulic model by building a pixel-precise deep learning flood forecasting model.

Finally, this project uses of cloud technologies to deploy a website equipped with flood mapping visualization and a live user alert system. The design shows great promise in the use of deep learning to produce modern flood forecasting and inundation models that can automate future flood detection processes.
